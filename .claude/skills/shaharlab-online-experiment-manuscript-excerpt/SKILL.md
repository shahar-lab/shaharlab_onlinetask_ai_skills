# Skill: Shahar Lab Online Experiment Manuscript Excerpt

## When to use
Invoke via `/shaharlab-online-experiment-manuscript-excerpt` when the researcher wants the task procedure written up for a manuscript. This drives Baruch (`manuscript-editor-baruch`).

## The output is a paragraph, not a report

The deliverable is **one dense narrative paragraph** (or a short run of paragraphs if the design has multiple conditions/experiments — see Example 2) describing exactly how the task worked, in the style a method section actually reads in print. No headers, no bullet lists, no tables, no Participants/Design/Stimuli/Data-Recording subsections. Everything — cover story, trial structure, response keys, timing, feedback, what was randomized vs. fixed, counterbalancing, and how understanding was verified — gets woven into flowing prose, third-person, past tense (or future tense if the experiment hasn't run yet — see Example 2).

### Example 1 (single condition)
> Participants played a "Casino Card Game" in which they were asked to select cards to maximize their monetary rewards. They were informed that on each trial, a Casino machine would randomly present a pair of cards from a deck of three pairs (i.e., six cards), with the limitation that the same pair could not be offered in two subsequent trials. Participants were asked to choose one of the two offered cards by pressing a response key that corresponded to the card's location on the screen ('S' response key for the left card and 'K' for the right card; up to 6 seconds). After selection, both cards disappeared, and a fixation appeared for 500 ms (choice feedback), followed by a 1000 ms outcome display at the center of the screen, in which participants were provided with feedback on whether they won (golden coin) or lost (red coin) money in the current trial. Critically, only the cards determined the winning chances, while the cards' locations and associated response keys were assigned each trial at random by the computer and were completely irrelevant to outcome prediction. Importantly, this fact was clearly conveyed to participants using instructions and a dedicated quiz (see below).

### Example 2 (multiple conditions/experiments)
> Across three experiments, participants will play a "Casino Card Game" in which they have to select options to maximize their monetary rewards. In each experiment, the task will include two between-subject conditions, differing in how the game will be structured. In the spatial-motor outcome-irrelevant condition, participants will be presented with four colored cards (green, red, purple, and blue). On each trial, two of the colored cards will be offered for choice and will be randomly assigned to two of four fixed rectangular screen locations: left, down, right, or up. The two unavailable locations will be filled with a grey card, indicating they could not be chosen on the current trial. Once a response is made, all options disappear, and a fixation screen is displayed for 750 ms. This is followed by probabilistic feedback (either 0 coin or +1 coin), presented for 1000 ms. Importantly, participants will be instructed that the casino machine randomly allocates the colored cards to two locations on each trial. Location allocation will indeed be random, ensuring that the outcome (+1 or 0 golden coins) depends solely on the color of the chosen card, and not on its location or the response key selected.
>
> In the visual outcome-irrelevant condition, participants will see the same trial sequence, yet only the spatial location (or the selected response key) will determine the outcomes and not the color of the cards. Specifically, participants will be asked to choose between four locations (left, up, right, or down), rather than between colored cards. On each trial, two locations will be available for choice as marked by a colored card appearing in them. The two unavailable locations will be filled with a grey card, indicating they are unavailable on the current trial. Thus, participants will have to attend to the colors during action selection to identify which locations are available for choice. Color-location pairings will be fully randomized on every trial, and participants will be instructed that the casino machine randomly allocates two colored cards to indicate the available locations. Similarly to how the locations are randomized in the spatial-motor outcome-irrelevant condition, here the colored cards are randomized and serve only as cues for the available locations, carrying no reward-relevant information. Thus, trial sequences will be completely identical between the conditions, only differing in how instructions and reward probabilities are framed.
>
> The probability of reward will depend on the expected value of the chosen relevant feature (i.e., color in the spatial-motor outcome-irrelevant condition or location in the visual outcome-irrelevant condition), which drifts slowly across trials using a random walk. The same two random walk sets will be used for the two conditions across all three experiments, with the two sets randomly counterbalanced between participants. Moreover, the assignment of reward probabilities to specific colors (in the spatial-motor outcome-irrelevant condition) and locations (in the visual outcome-irrelevant condition) will be randomized across participants, such that the same color or location will be associated with different reward probabilities for different individuals.
>
> Each participant will complete 200 trials of one of the conditions, with short breaks every 50 trials to minimize fatigue. To incentivize performance, participants will be instructed to try to maximize their earnings and will receive a monetary bonus based on their final score. Instructions will clearly state that in the spatial-motor outcome-irrelevant condition, only the color of the card predicts reward, and in the spatial-motor condition, only the location matters for reward outcomes. Participants' understanding of the instructions will be validated using a dedicated quiz, which they will have to answer with 100% accuracy to start the task (see SI).
>
> In both conditions, participants will make their selection with the middle and index fingers of the left and right hands using the arrow keys on a QWERTY keyboard (left, down, right, and up), within a 6-second response window. Thus, across the two conditions the spatial feature and the motor feature used to indicate choice are tightly aligned (e.g., pressing the left arrow key for choosing the left option).

## Process

1. **Read the project.** Baruch reads the plan (`ai_artifacts/plan/EXPERIMENT_BLUEPRINT.md`, `ai_artifacts/plan/artifacts/SPECIFICATION.md`), instructions, and task code — enough to know the cover story, trial sequence, timing constants, response device, what's randomized/counterbalanced, and how comprehension is checked. See his agent definition for the full read list.
2. **Write the paragraph(s).** Only from what's actually in the code/spec — no invented numbers, no invented cover story details. Match the register and density of the examples above: specific numbers inline, mechanism explained (what's randomized, what predicts outcome, what's irrelevant and why the participant is told so), no meta-commentary.
3. **Flag gaps inline**, don't guess: `[CONFIRM WITH RESEARCHER: fixation duration is 500ms in one file and 1000ms in another — which is current?]`
4. **Save the output** to `ai_artifacts/manuscript-excerpt/method_excerpt.md`. Create the folder if missing. Never modify any other project file.
5. **Report** to the researcher: where the file is, and a one-line pointer to any `[CONFIRM WITH RESEARCHER: ...]` markers that need their input.
